cARR · Executive Proposal Context
The GTM instrument for the seat → consumption pivot
Rajesh Kadam · April 2026

SaaS pricing is moving from seats to consumption.
Our measurement system has to move with it.

AI is the forcing function — per-token cost structure makes per-seat licensing a mismatch, and the rest of the category is repricing on consumption this year. Committed ARR was built for the seat era; in the consumption era it is silent on five specific places the signed dollar does not equal the earned dollar.
The pivot, in one line
  • Seat era: price is a function of licenses; the close is the outcome.
  • Consumption era: price is a function of usage; the close is the start of the outcome.
  • The measurement system a seat-based book relies on (Committed ARR) reads every signed dollar as earned. That is a workable approximation under seat pricing. It is not under consumption.
Five places the assumption breaks — and Committed ARR is silent
  • Shelfware — signed, not consuming. Seat-based books full ACV.
  • Spike-drop — heavy early use, then the cliff. Still books full ACV until expiry.
  • Overage — consuming beyond entitlement. Seat-based has no line item for this.
  • Mid-term expansion — second contract compounds. Priced line-by-line, compounding invisible.
  • Orphaned usage — rogue consumption with no contract match. No hook in seat-based accounting.
cARR is a bounded, rules-based read that makes all five failure modes visible in dollars — without changing what the board sees or how Committed ARR rolls up.

Proposal

cARR — the instrument built to see the five failure modes the pivot creates.

A single dollar-denominated figure that preserves Committed ARR's rollup and auditability, and applies a bounded, rules-based HealthScore that reflects the five consumption-era failure modes — in a form a rep can read and a CFO can defend.

cARR  =  Committed_ARR  ×  HealthScore
HealthScore ∈ [0.40, 1.30] — bounded, interpretable, rules-based — spec 03 v0.7.1
Bounded
Worst case is known before the pivot.
HealthScore floor 0.40, ceiling 1.30. No single account — shelfware or explosive — can move a rep's attainment by more than the cap, a property a seat-based comp plan takes for granted and a naive consumption score does not.
Rules-based
Publishable during a pricing pivot.
SQL rules, not a learned model. A rep in a comp dispute can read the line that moved her HealthScore. A learned score during a pricing pivot is a double black box: pricing changed, comp changed, and neither is explainable.
Fair to new logos
No adoption signal, no penalty.
Accounts with no usage posted yet default to HealthScore = 1.00 — full booking credit during the pre-signal window. A single-line rule (D12b), not a four-parameter ramp curve to fight over every comp cycle.
Additive
The seat-era rollup survives.
cARR sits alongside Committed ARR on the existing rollup. Committed ARR continues to report to the board unchanged — we are not asking finance or audit to migrate off their anchor during the pivot.
Auditable — every rule cites a written spec section Additive — rolls up by rep, region, segment, and time Bounded — worst-case account-level impact is known pre-deployment

cARR · Executive Proposal Considerations
Considerations specific to a pricing pivot

Four decisions a measurement change during a pricing pivot has to earn.

The risk during a pivot is not the math — it is that pricing and comp change at once, and the field loses trust. Each of these was vetted against the spec and the eval framework before review; the mitigations are in the pipeline today, not on a roadmap.

Field trust when pricing and comp are both moving.
A single-cycle shadow-comp window precedes any change in paid compensation. Both figures appear on the monthly rep statement; comp remains paid on Committed ARR. Automated rollback to Committed-based comp if drift exceeds the agreed threshold or any T4/T5 eval fails. Because the rule is SQL — not a learned model — a rep in a dispute can read the exact line that moved her HealthScore.
Why bounded rules, not a learned score, during the pivot.
During a pricing pivot a learned HealthScore is a double black box: the pricing changed, the comp driver changed, and the weights are not defensible line-by-line. Bounded rules keep the metric publishable, auditable, and reversible in one PR. A learned prioritization layer is proposed for v1.5 — on top of rule-based cARR, not inside it — once labeled outcomes from shadow comp are available.
Proof the metric still sees the pivot's failure modes.
Sixteen automated evals across five tiers — including the new T5 Transition fidelity tier, which asserts per failure mode (shelfware, spike-drop, overage, expansion, orphans) that cARR's dollar answer diverges from a seat-based read in the expected direction and magnitude. T1, T4, and T5 are stop-the-line: a single regression on a pricing-pivot failure mode halts deployment, no exceptions.
New-logo fairness, without a tuning fight mid-pivot.
D12b — pre-signal trust. When an account has no usage posted yet (U is undefined), HealthScore = 1.00 by default — full booking credit during the pre-signal window. A one-line rule with zero tuning parameters, replacing the earlier v0.6 four-knob ramp blend that every comp cycle would re-litigate. During a pricing pivot, fewer knobs is the whole point.

cARR · Executive Proposal Methodology
How this was built — for a pricing pivot

Spec-first, with an AI coding assistant in the loop.

A measurement change during a pricing pivot has to be traceable, reversible, and provably doing the job. Every artifact — metric, pipeline, tests, dashboard — was developed against a written specification with explicit decision records. The assistant accelerated drafting; a human signed off on every merge.

01 · Specifications and decision records

The argument before the code.

Twelve markdown specifications, thirteen architecture decision records (ADRs — including one supersedence, D12→D12b) in a shared decision log. Every code file cites the spec section it implements, and parameter changes require a spec amendment in the same pull request — the discipline a pricing pivot depends on.

  • Spec 03 v0.7.1 — the formula, new-logo fairness rule, and 2026-04-21 calibration
  • Spec 05 — data-quality catalog (16 assertions)
  • Spec 06 v0.2 — eval framework, now five tiers including T5 Transition fidelity (16 checks)
  • Spec 11 — the AI product surface
02 · AI-assisted drafting, human judgment

Speed from the assistant, safety from review.

Approximately 70% of boilerplate — SQL scaffolds, test assertions, schema documentation — was drafted by the assistant. The material errors were caught by humans running the code against real sandboxes and reading failing evals.

D09: An assistant-generated MONTH-partitioned BQ schema interacted with the sandbox’s 60-day expiration policy, silently evicting 77% of rows on load. Caught at integration, not unit test.
D12→D12b: Version 0.5 of the formula produced a uniform HealthScore haircut for every new logo. A dedicated new-logo eval failed. The v0.6 fix added a four-parameter segment-aware ramp blend; v0.7 supersedes it with a single-line pre-signal trust rule — fewer knobs, same fairness guarantee, the right direction for a pivot.

Commit-level provenance is preserved: every commit carries a Co-Authored-By trailer identifying the assistant and the model.

03 · The AI layer as a product surface

Ask cARR — a bounded NL query layer.

The natural-language query layer is not a thin wrapper around a model. It is a five-stage pipeline, each stage independently testable:

  • Retriever — mart schema hint
  • Generator — Claude produces a SQL candidate
  • Verifier — local EXPLAIN dry-run prior to execution
  • Narrator — second-pass summary of the result set
  • Refusal policy — PII, forecasting, cross-rep comp refused explicitly

The generated SQL is shown alongside every answer. The pipeline is the system of record; the model is a query interface.

cARR · Executive Proposal Recommendation
Align the measurement system with the pricing system — in phases

A three-phase rollout with explicit gates.

No phase commits additional compensation liability until the previous phase has produced clean data for one full cycle. T5 Transition fidelity and T4 Comp safety are stop-the-line gates at every phase. Rollback at any stage is a single decision, supported by the pipeline.

PHASE 1
This quarter

Publish alongside.

cARR joins Committed ARR on the executive dashboard as a second read of the book — the consumption-era complement to the seat-era anchor. Read-only. No compensation impact. Field leaders and reps see both figures side-by-side for one full review cycle.

Gate to Phase 2: all T4 and T5 evals green for 90 days; pivot failure modes visibly surfacing.

PHASE 2
Next quarter

Shadow comp.

Compensation is dual-computed monthly. Both figures appear on the rep statement. Comp is still paid on Committed ARR. The objective is to surface the divergence on specific accounts and have reps learn to read the five pivot failure modes before a dollar of comp moves.

Gate to Phase 3: dual-comp delta within the pre-agreed band, no T4/T5 failure, VPS and CFO sign-off.

PHASE 3
Q+2

Cutover with automated rollback.

cARR becomes the driver of variable compensation — the measurement system now aligned with the consumption-era pricing system. Committed ARR continues to report to the board unchanged. Automated rollback to Committed-based comp if drift exceeds 5% or any T4/T5 eval fails — executed by the pipeline, without a manual migration.

Safety net: one-command revert; no data migration required.

Recommendation — three decisions from this review

  1. Approve Phase 1 — publish cARR alongside Committed ARR this quarter; make the five pivot failure modes visible before the comp plan is touched.
  2. Name a VPS delegate and a CFO delegate to the working group — one point of contact each; co-own the T5 threshold review every quarter.
  3. In-principle approval for shadow comp — contingent on Phase 1 gates being met; the pricing pivot timeline should not race ahead of the measurement pivot.
